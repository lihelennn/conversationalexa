Introduction to Conversational AI


Total Time: 10-15 minutes

Goal: Introduce students to AI and conversational AI. Students will draw upon their experience with AI (in real life or through TV/movies/books) to understand important features of and challenges related to conversational agents. Demo voice assistant in classroom.

Materials: slides, Siri/Alexa/Bixby-capable phone

Ethics Discussion: Can Alexa understand everyone equally? Why might this be an issue? How does Alexa find us information? What kind of information does Alexa collect from us? Should we trust Alexa?

Teacher’s Guide: Annotated copy of slides with key points to emphasize in presentation.

Introduction - 3 minutes:
What is and isn’t AI? Classification activity based on student opinions. In definition of AI, communicate that at the core of any AI system is the ability to learn from data.

“AI that can communicate with us in a natural way is called conversational AI. Where have you seen conversational AI?”

Demo Voice Assistant - 5 minutes:

“We’ve brought along a little helper to teach us about conversational AI. Let’s meet her:" ‹ ask agent what their name is, how are they?›

“Besides asking about our conversational agent, we can also ask her to help us learn and find resources” ‹ ask agent a question›

Ethics Discussion - 5 minutes:

“Where do you think this information is coming from? What makes an agent’s communication natural vs. robotic? Why might this matter?”

‹Ask questions in different ways, ask confusing questions.›
"What might affect the agent’s ability to understand what we ask her? Can she understand everyone equally?”

“Clearly, apart from factors out of our control (ex: accent), we as users can understand how to ask good questions to get the information we need. Let’s think about how through a game you might be familiar with: 20 questions!”

Unplugged Activity: 20 Questions


Total Time: 15-20 minutes

Goal: Students will think about the extent to which the way we ask questions influences the kind of answers we get in return.

Materials: worksheet

Ethics Discussion: How does this differ from the kinds of questions we can ask and the answers we can get from an agent? Are there limitations on that too?

Teacher’s Guide: Explanation of how to play 20 Questions. Sample student worksheet diagramming out question asking/answering process.

Set-up - 2 minutes:
Distribute Q&A mapping worksheets (1 per pair). Have students pair up, explain rules: one student asks, other answers (yes/no questions only).

Game Play - 10 minutes:
In the left columns of the worksheet, the answerer writes down questions asked. On the right, the asker writes down the response. At the end of 20 questions, asker writes down the final guess and the answerer writes down the final answer.
Circle to ensure students understand what’s happening and are reminded of the key ‘big picture’ questions prompted by the activity.

Debrief - 5 minutes:
Bring the whole class together.

“Raise your hand if you asked questions. If so, did you ask a random question each time or narrow down what to ask based on the responses? Was it important for your partner to understand what you were asking?"

"Raise your hand if you answered questions. Did you feel like your answers were helpful every time? What made it hard to answer questions?"

How did our game differ from how we interacted with our conversational agent? Do you think there are challenges to interacting with conversational agents too?”

Ethics Discussion - 5 minutes:

"How does this differ from the kinds of questions we can ask and the answers we can get from an agent? Are there limitations on that too?"

Unplugged Activity: Voiceflow Prototype


Total Time: 50 minutes

Goal: Students will be able to create a template with VoiceFlow blocks and explain how the flow works; students will make their own voice flow prototype for certain prompts

Materials: paper, pencil, scissors

Ethics Discussion: At the end, describing what information Alexa needs to know even in these paper prototypes and how Alexa needs information too!

Teacher’s Guide: What each block does, how the template works (including an example on how to put the blocks together), example of a solution to Hello World, 3-5 Questions to Alexa prompt, takeaway from the activity

Introduction and explaining Voiceflow diagram - 5 minutes minutes:
Bring the whole class together.

“We will now be creating our own voice assistants using paper and pencil. Here, we have Voiceflow diagrams. Voiceflow is a way to code your own Alexas by asking questions, drawing out tree diagrams and flow diagrams so that Alexa knows how to respond.”

Example: this is how to use Voiceflow. If I want to teach Alexa what my favorite color is, here is an example of how to do it.

Pointpoint Presentation

Student's Turn - Create an Alexa that will greet me back - 7 minutes:

“Now it’s your turn! Code an Alexa that will respond hello or hi if I say hello or hi”

Student’s Turn - Create an Alexa that will greet me back - 15 minutes:

“Now it’s your turn! Code an Alexa that will answer 3-5 questions based on the information you provide Alexa with and how Alexa will interpret your question. For example: What class do I have at 12pm? What color shoes should I wear today? What animals are nocturnal?

Sharing Time - Create an Alexa that will greet me back - 15 minutes:
Bring class back together to share their findings.

Ethics Discussion: 15 minutes
Partner Work - 5 minutes: Put students in pairs to discuss these questions:
  • Why is it useful to map out each block and flow?
  • What do the students really have to teach Alexa even in these paper prototypes in order for Alexa to fully answer all the questions to her best knowledge?
  • What happens if Alexa doesn’t understand you or doesn’t know the answer?

Group Discussion: 10 minutes

“Now as a class, let’s discuss what you guys learned together. We will be writing your ideas down, so please share!”
“We asked you these questions because it is important to realize that even with these paper versions of Alexa, you still need to tell Alexa information! Voice assistants do not always know what you are saying or know the answer to what you are saying, so the power is really in your hands to code the world you are living in.”

App Inventor Tutorial


Total Time: 25 minutes

Goal: Students will learn that they will be creating their own capstone project with MIT App Inventor. Students will also receive a tutorial on how to use the program.

Materials: Computers/laptops with WiFi

Ethics Discussion: At the end, describing what information Alexa needs to know even in these paper prototypes and how Alexa needs information too!

Teacher’s Guide: Information on how to use App Inventor, possible notes provided by Jessica’s team, how to troubleshoot for any possible things that could go wrong for a WiFi intensive activity, takeaways from the activity, other App Inventor activities that teachers can incorporate in their daily lesson plans

Introduction and a Quick App Inventor Demo - 7 minutes

“After day 1, we hope that you all have a better understanding about how to code voice assistants such as Alexa. Now here’s the good and fun news: you will all actually be doing that with some coding! At the end of these five days, you will hopefully have enough skills to be able to code your own Alexa for a final project that you will all decide on. This project is meant to be for something useful in your community, so start brainstorming about topics or events that you really care about in any community you’re involved in. We will be doing more work on this later on.”

“Now, for the fun part. You will be coding these projects with MIT’s App Inventor, a platform where you can develop your own phone apps using some very simple blocks on the internet. That means that you all will learn how to code!”

“How many of you have coded before?”

“How many of you have coded with App Inventor before?”

“For those of you who have never used App Inventor before or for those who have but still need a refresher, we will be going through a demonstration now, and then we’ll give you a chance to get familiar with App Inventor before you do something related to Alexa with App Inventor.”

“Today, you will all be working on a tutorial made with the MIT App Inventor Team”

  • Hello Codi Tutorial Link
  • Have students go through steps 1-2 of the tutorial to describe the environment. Also, have students give examples of dragging and dropping, creating a button, changing the text.

Student’s Turn (Partner Work) - Go through remainder of Hello Codi Tutorial - 18 minutes:
Have students finish off the tutorial or get as far as they can. Encourage them to play around with the blocks and move onto the next step when done.

Empathy/ Audience-Center Design Activity


Total Time: 60 minutes

Goal: Students will be able to understand empathy and what empathy design entails, why you should care about empathy

Materials: worksheet, pencil, craft supplies (scissors, straws, fuzzy straw things, construction paper, yarn, wire, markers, crayons)

Ethics Discussion: At the end, discuss what ethical design is - talk about the difference between copying work versus being inspired by work, what is designing with empathy and how to do that, is Alexa empathic to the user, is Alexa an example of empathetic design? Discuss designing for social good/ accessibility.

Teacher’s Guide: What an empathy map is, how to fill out an empathy map, how to fill out the worksheet, resources to explain what empathy design is and what an empathy map is provided by creating a guide on it via google information or MIT Engineering Innovation and Design Guide

Interview - 2.5 minutes per person

According to the community problem prompt, write down anything that your partner has to say about the topic and objectives that you may want to accomplish based on their opinions about the topic - make sure to actively listen and maintain eye contact with your partner

Interview #2 - 2.5 minutes per person
Pick one or two topics mentioned from the first interview and have them talk more in detail about it and what they might want in terms of a possible solution - make sure to actively listen and maintain eye contact with your partner

Capture findings - 5 minutes
Write down the needs of your partner: what is your partner trying to achieve and what are the problems and issues you are trying to solve, write down your findings (findings from interview #2). Write down the insights of your partner: what needs, objectives, motivations, or insights do they have that were not explicitly expressed.

Problem Statement - 2.5 minutes
Write down the problem statement - your partner’s needs because they care about/concerned with insight.

Ideate, generate several solutions - 5 minutes
Sketch three innovative solutions to the problem that meets your partner’s needs

>Feedback and iterate idea - 2.5 minutes per person
Write down what your partnered liked and did not like about your solutions when you explain the solutions you created

Generate new solution - 5 minutes
Create a new solution that improves upon your original solution and sketch the new improved solution

Build you solution - 10 or 15 minutes
With craft supplies, build the solution

Share solution and get feedback #2: 5 minutes per person
Present the crafted solution and write down feedback from the partner, what worked, what could be improved, questions they have about the product, and ideas they have as a result of the solution.

Ethics Discussion - 5 minutes:

"What is designing with empathy? What are some inportant factors when interviewing your partner?" "What is ethical design? Where is the line drawn between getting inspiration and copying someone's work" "Is Alexa an example of empathetic design? Is Alexa an example of ethical design?" "How do we incorporate accessibility into design? Explain what the design for good encapsulates. Design thinking can have an impace on good desert and access to quality nutrition, homelessness, immigration, race & gender equality to name a few"

K-Script Activity


Total Time: 35-45 minutes

Goal: Students will be able to visualize and design their speaking skill with highlighted importance on the interaction between the system and the user and how to create contingency plans for when things do not go as expected or if Alexa doesn’t understand you. The K-script can be edited during the project process, iterating on their speaking skill design.

Materials: worksheet, pencil

Ethics Discussion: what is authentic relationship with an AI system. Talk about the history of AI chatbots (Eliza) where research found that humans were more likely to confide in Eliza than a human psychologist, what is the turing test?

Teacher’s Guide: MIT’s Engineer Innovation and Design Guide, or created guide based on information found on Google- explaining what K-scripting is, why it’s useful, how to fill out the worksheet, what contingency plans are and what they look like.

K-Scripting
Distribute worksheet, in groups of 1-2 fill out worksheet with particular speaking skill idea in mind for the capstone project

Instructions:
In groups of 1-2, create a dialogue of how you would interact with Alexa or your favorite voice assistant. This will help you design and think about how you would like to create your own speaking skill for your capstone project. Think of it as a short movie script with you as the actor/actress and Alexa as your co-actor in the scene. You will want to describe what Alexa will be saying, what you are saying, and what is happening in the scene that we cannot see and what we can see.
User - the User is either you or Alexa
Observable Actions - what you can see, this would be the dialogue or any sounds that you or Alexa will be making
Unobservable Actions - things that you cannot see, like you getting out of bed in the morning or looking off into space
Things to keep in mind:
Contingency Plans - when you prepare for things that may go wrong or not what you expect will happen. For example, what should you do if Alexa doesn’t understand your response? What if Alexa interpreters your response correctly, but makes the wrong decision, what should you do? You may incorporate this into your plan, if you have time!
Debriefing: 10 minutes
Each group will present their script to their class, acting it out with one user being Alexa, the other students being the user. Discuss what worked and what didn’t, challenges they came across.

Ethics - 10 minutes:
Bring the whole class together.

“What does an authentic relationship with Alexa mean to you?"

"Raise your hand if you answered questions. Did you feel like your answers were helpful every time? What made it hard to answer questions?"

Discuss brief history of chatbots: Eliza. The research study showed that the human subjects were able to confide in Eliza more than a human psychologist. What does the interaction with Eliza suggest about the interactions between AI and humans?

"What is the turing test?""
